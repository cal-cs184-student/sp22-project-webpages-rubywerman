Project 4: Cloth Sim

OVERVIEW

In this project we created a cloth simulation using masses and springs. We started by creating the cloth by implementing a system of point masses and springs. We divided the cloth into point masses and connected them together with springs. Next, we added the physical equations of motion to represent gravity and spring forces on the cloth. We used verlet integration to compute new point mass positions. Then we implemented collisions with spheres and planes. Adding collision support enabled us to drape the cloth over a sphere in a realistic manner. After that we handled self-collisions, which allowed us to represent the cloth falling/folding on itself realistically. Finally, we created shader programs, which allowed us to speed up sections of the graphics pipeline. We recreated diffuse shading, Blinn-Phong shading, texture mapping, displacement mapping, bump mapping, and environment-mapped reflections in shader programs. One challenge we ran into was creating the shader programs. It was challenging at first to learn the syntax, but we read the documentation and got familiar with it.

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

Density = 15, Ks = 10

Since the low spring constant is making the cloth less rigid, the folds in the cloth are much smaller compared to the default settings. This also increases the number of folds in the cloth since more can fit in the cloth.

Density = 100, Ks = 5000

With a higher density, the weight of the cloth overcomes the rigidity of the default spring constant of 5000. This also results in much smaller folds and a higher number of them in the cloth.

Density = 10, Ks = 5000

With a lower density and default spring constant, the folds in the cloth are much larger and propagate up the cloth much quicker. The higher rigidity with respect to density allows the cloth to keep its shape better, and does not allow many smaller folds to form.

Part 5: Shaders

Shaders are programs that run individual parts of the rasterization pipeline parallel on the GPU. The purpose of shader programs is to speed up the rasterization progress. Vertex shaders operate on vertices. These transformations modify the geometric properties of vertices like position and normal vectors. The fragment shaders are what we are left with after rasterization. We use elements that we calculate by the vertex shader to calculate the final out_color. We must use vertex and fragment shaders together to create a shader program. We use the output of the vertex shader as the input of the fragment shader.

The Blinn-Phong shading model is a per-sample method that we can use to make the shading of three dimensional scenes look more realistic with respect to the lighting and shading of objects. The model has three components: an ambient constant term, a diffuse term, and a specular term that depends on how the material reflects light.
